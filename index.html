Though the modern English concept of 'emotion' has a specific and comparatively short history, as Thomas Dixon has emphasized, as a category it also has members that previously belonged to the categories of 'passion' or 'sentiment,' even though these three categories are not coextensive. Other cultures too have and have had categories that share some or many of the features of these English-language categories: the extensive list of pathē given by Aristotle in Book 2 of his Rhetoric consists very largely of items which, even if they would not all necessarily occur to us as prototypical 'best examples' of emotion, would still fall straightforwardly under that category, and which, even if they do not coincide precisely with their nearest English equivalents in sense, reference, extension, and connotation, all have near-equivalents or analogues in English which would be regarded by the average English-speaker as emotion words. This remains true regardless of the facts that (a) pathos in Greek covers a much wider semantic range than English 'emotion' (as Peter Singer explores further in this volume), and (b) even the more specific pathē (or pathēmata) of the psuchē cover more than the forms of experience that we call 'emotions.' The prototypical cases of these pathē of the psuchē, however (as offered, for example, in Aristotle's De anima 1.1 and especially in Rhetoric 2), are largely what we should describe as emotions. The same holds true for analogous concepts in Latin such as animi motus, adfectio, adfectus, passio, and perturbatio. These terms also resemble the English 'emotion' in another way: they highlight the element of phenomenological passivity—the sense that emotions are experiences that happen to us rather than actions that we perform—that is a typical feature of emotion concepts.
